# Website Discovery Questions
## Bila Salehuddin & Co. — Interaction Design & Brand Alignment
*A guided conversation for your design review meeting*

---

> **How to use this:** These aren't meant to be read out one by one. Think of them as conversation anchors — start with a question, let it breathe, and follow where the client takes it. The sections move from the big picture down to specific moments on the site.

---

## Part 1 — Brand Personality & First Impression
*Goal: Understand what feeling the firm wants to project, and whether the site currently delivers it.*

1. When a potential client lands on the homepage for the very first time — what is the single emotion or impression you want them to walk away with? Not the information, but the *feeling.*

2. The site currently leans into a prestigious, formal aesthetic — dark navy, gold accents, serif headings. Does that palette feel true to how the firm presents itself in person, or does it feel more aspirational than accurate?

3. The tagline "Peguambela & Peguamcara · Advocates & Solicitors" opens the hero. When you imagine your ideal client reading that, do you think they feel welcomed and oriented — or does it put distance between them and you before you've said anything of substance?

4. Your firm's stated voice is "no padding, no posturing — plain language you can act on." Looking at the homepage right now, how close does the writing feel to that promise? Where does it drift?

5. The banner background uses a glass skyscraper photograph — corporate and architectural. Does that image feel like *your* firm, or more like a generic law firm stock photo? What image, if any, comes to mind when you think of the firm's real environment?

---

## Part 2 — User Journey & Navigation Logic
*Goal: Surface friction between how users are expected to move through the site and what the brand is actually communicating.*

6. Think about the three most common types of people who will visit this site. Walk me through what you imagine each of them is trying to do within the first 30 seconds — and then tell me honestly: does the current homepage make that path obvious?

7. The navigation currently shows: Home, About, Eviction, Contracts, Contact, and Book Consultation. The firm actually handles six practice areas — but four of them (Conveyancing, Litigation, IP, Accidents) aren't in the nav. If a client arrived because of an accident case, how confident are you they'd find their way to relevant information?

8. The site has two places to "get in touch" — the Contact page with a form, and the Book Consultation page with WhatsApp buttons. Do you think clients understand the difference between those two paths? Or might the duplication create hesitation about which one to use?

9. On the homepage, a visitor scrolls through: hero → practice areas → why us → team → clients → offices → CTA. Does that sequence feel like a natural argument that builds toward action, or does it feel like information stacking up without a clear pull toward the next step?

10. When a client finishes reading about, say, Eviction Matters — the only next step offered is "Book a Consultation." Are there cases where a client might want a softer next step — something between "I'm just reading" and "I'm ready to commit to a meeting"?

---

## Part 3 — Interactive Elements & Touchpoints
*Goal: Identify moments where the interaction design contradicts or weakens the brand promise.*

11. The Book Consultation page now routes through WhatsApp. The brand positions itself as premium and formal — gold accents, serif fonts, Malaysian Bar credentials. Does sending a new client into a WhatsApp chat feel consistent with that positioning, or does it feel like a gear shift downward?

12. When someone selects a meeting type (Eviction, Contracts, General) on the booking page — the WhatsApp message pre-fills automatically. Did you know that was happening? And does it feel helpful, or does it feel presumptuous on behalf of the client?

13. The contact form on the Contact page asks for name, phone, email, matter type, and a brief description — then has a PDPA consent checkbox. Imagine a stressed landlord filling this in at 11pm because their tenant just refused to leave. Does that form feel like it's on their side, or does it feel like admin?

14. The four practice cards on the homepage (Conveyancing, Litigation, IP, Accidents) don't link anywhere — they're display-only, while Eviction and Contracts do link to full pages. If a client clicks on "Intellectual Property" and nothing happens, what does that communicate about those practice areas?

15. The WhatsApp floating button appears on every page. In the context of the About page — while a client is reading a senior partner's biography — does a floating green chat bubble reinforce the brand, or interrupt it?

16. The FAQ accordions on the Eviction and Contracts pages expand and collapse on click. Is there any content the firm regularly gets asked about that *isn't* in those FAQs right now? What's the question clients ask most often that the site currently doesn't answer?

---

## Part 4 — Brand Promise vs. Delivered Experience
*Goal: Find gaps between what the messaging promises and what the site actually delivers.*

17. The site says "you work with the lawyer handling your matter — no layers, no handoffs." But the team section shows four people with placeholder silhouettes and no photographs. Does the absence of real faces undermine the personal, direct-access promise the copy is making?

18. The About page introduces all five team members with full biographies. But the homepage team section only shows four of them. Is there a reason Sufiea Binti Aripin — the firm's Legal Assistant — is not featured on the homepage? And does that feel intentional or like an oversight?

19. The site lists nine notable clients including IYRES, Speedhome, and STV Japan — this is genuinely impressive work. But the client section is just a grid of pill-shaped name badges with no context. Does that presentation do justice to the scope of that work, or is it leaving impact on the table?

20. The firm's Malaysian Bar registration number and certificate date appear on the About page. That detail builds credibility — but it's buried three scrolls deep on a secondary page. Should any of that trust signal be visible earlier in the journey, say on the homepage or in the footer?

21. The footer currently lists six practice areas in a small column of text. A visitor seeing "Conveyancing" and "Litigation" as footer links that don't click anywhere may feel like the site is unfinished. How important is it to you that every practice area has its own dedicated page?

---

## Part 5 — Audience & Tone Calibration
*Goal: Make sure the interaction design is calibrated to the actual emotional state of the target client.*

22. The firm handles eviction matters, accident claims, and criminal litigation alongside corporate contracts and conveyancing. That's a wide emotional spectrum — from anxious landlords to corporate legal teams. Does the site's current tone serve all of those people equally, or is it tilted toward one type of client?

23. If a client is coming to the site after a distressing event — a car accident, a tenant who won't leave, or a criminal matter involving a family member — does the site's current aesthetic and language make them feel safe and understood, or does it feel detached?

24. The site is bilingual in branding (the Malay name "Tetuan" and "Peguambela & Peguamcara" appear prominently) but the copy is entirely in English. Is that a deliberate choice for your primary audience? And are there clients who would expect or prefer Bahasa Malaysia anywhere on the site?

25. The brand voice is confident and stripped-back ("We don't pretend to do everything"). That voice works well for a corporate client who values directness. Does it also work for someone who is emotionally vulnerable and looking for reassurance alongside competence?

---

## Part 6 — Cohesion & What's Missing
*Goal: Identify the gap between the site as it stands and the site as it should be.*

26. After looking through the full site — is there a page, a section, or a piece of content that you feel is conspicuously absent? Something that you find yourself explaining verbally to potential clients that the site doesn't show?

27. The site has no testimonials, no case outcomes, and no press mentions. For a firm that has worked with government agencies, Japanese corporations, and national research bodies — is there a reason social proof hasn't been incorporated? Is it a policy choice, or simply not yet done?

28. If you were a competitor looking at this site, what is the one thing you'd most want to copy — and what is the one weakness you'd try to exploit?

29. Looking at everything — the visual design, the brand language, the way people move through the site, the booking experience — what is the single thing that feels most *off* to you right now? The thing that, if fixed, would make the rest of it feel right.

30. Finally: when the site is exactly where you want it to be, how will you know? What will a client say — or do — that tells you the site is working?
